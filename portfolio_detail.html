EMS Response Times in NYC

This project investigates the impact of a neighborhood's economic wealth on access to emergency medical services. Focusing on New York City and its five boroughs—Manhattan, Queens, the Bronx, Brooklyn, and Staten Island—we hypothesized that lower-income boroughs face more barriers in obtaining efficient emergency medical response resources compared to higher-income boroughs. New York City serves as an ideal case study due to its significant wealth inequalities and densely populated areas that clearly delineate wealthy and impoverished neighborhoods. By analyzing data from 2021, we also considered the effects of COVID-19 on emergency medical services and demographic trends in each borough. This study provides insights relevant to other urban areas in the United States with similar socioeconomic disparities.

General Outline of our Project:

  • We first cleaned three data sets, one API related to borough demographics, one .json file regarding average emergency response times for each month across several years, and one large .csv file with ~24 million rows of every EMS case in NYC from 2005 to halfway through 2022. We then proceeded to analyze these data sets in a specific order:
  • First we used the API related to borough demographic data in order to predict 2021’s borough demographic data.
  • Next, we used the large .csv file to analyze the relationship between dispatch response times and incident response times based on various factors, and then we used the .json file to just analyze the center and spread of incident response times in more detail.

Insights

  • The Bronx & Brooklyn have the lowest median household incomes and highest poverty rates while Manhattan & Staten Island have the highest median incomes and lowest poverty rates in 2021.
  • The lowest percent of EMS incidents treated and transported fall under lower-income boroughs such as Brooklyn and Bronx.
  • Lower income boroughs had the greatest percentage of their cases where actual response time was less than predicted as opposed to higher income boroughs such as Manhattan and Staten Island.
  • Lower income boroughs have a higher mean and median incident response time as compared to higher income boroughs.
  • The spread of data varies significantly from borough to borough.

Conclusion

I also learned about the importance of visualizing data to find patterns and make insights regarding these patterns. Data Science is important not only to make and substantiate claims, but also to make informed decisions, understanding trends, predicting and forecasting, and constantly improving!
